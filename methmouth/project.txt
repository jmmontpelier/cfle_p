Once an officer has secured a phone and eliminated any potential physical threats, however, data on the phone can endanger no one.” It then went on to say \"[o]ur holding, of course, is not that the information on a cell phone is immune from search; it is instead that a warrant is generally required before such a search, even when a cell phone is seized incident to arrest.\"  Mobile phones can contain a great deal of private information, including private messages and photographs, information that is protected by the 4th Amendment. Such phones, which are essentially hand-held computers with communications and other capabilities, may also link to remote computers, thus providing access to an unlimited amount of private information. Therefore, in general, you must obtain a warrant before searching a mobile phone. Of course, as with any search, there may be exceptions to the warrant requirement, but no such exceptions seem to exist here.  For example, the scenario does not suggest that there is any kind of emergency which might justify an immediate warrantless search.  Therefore, you must get a warrant before searching the phone.
